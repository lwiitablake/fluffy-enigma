OER/ZTC COURSE DESIGN

Beyond the Prompt

A scalable model for responsible AI use in course design.

Use AI as a drafting and thinking partner while keeping human judgment, accessibility, privacy, equity, and student learning at the center.

Quick Summary

Responsible AI use is not just about writing better prompts. It is a repeatable workflow: define the problem, gather context, draft carefully, review with human judgment, revise, and document decisions.

The Scalable Workflow

Use this model whenever AI supports OER/ZTC materials, Canvas pages, assignments, assessments, examples, rubrics, or faculty development resources.

Step 1

Define the Problem

Identify the specific course design need before choosing a tool or writing a prompt.

Step 2

Gather Context

Use course outcomes, student needs, assignment purpose, accessibility expectations, and OER/ZTC constraints.

Step 3

Draft With AI

Use AI to generate a first draft, alternative wording, examples, outlines, or checklists.

Step 4

Review Carefully

Check the output for accuracy, accessibility, privacy, bias, alignment, tone, and usefulness.

Step 5

Revise and Document

Make human improvements and note what was changed, why, and what still needs review.

Core Question

Do not ask only, “Can AI do this?” Ask: Should AI help with this, what must a human review, and what could go wrong for students if we miss something?

Decision Cards

Choose the situation closest to what you are trying to do.

Clarifying Instructions

AI can help with: simplifying directions, organizing steps, and creating student-friendly wording.

Human review: confirm the instructions match the actual assignment, grading expectations, due dates, and Canvas setup.

Red flag: AI may invent steps, requirements, or submission details.

Accessibility Review

AI can help with: identifying possible barriers, suggesting alt text drafts, and checking for plain language.

Human review: verify heading structure, link purpose, captions, table structure, color contrast, and actual file accessibility.

Red flag: AI may say something is accessible without truly checking it.

Creating Examples

AI can help with: drafting sample scenarios, practice questions, and discipline-specific examples.

Human review: check accuracy, cultural assumptions, reading level, relevance, and whether examples reflect diverse student experiences.

Red flag: AI may default to narrow, stereotyped, or overly generic examples.

OER/ZTC Materials

AI can help with: summarizing open materials, drafting reading guides, and aligning resources to outcomes.

Human review: verify licensing, attribution needs, source accuracy, and whether the resource is actually usable for students.

Red flag: AI may misstate license terms or invent citations.

Rubrics and Feedback

AI can help with: drafting rubric language, feedback stems, and revision-focused comments.

Human review: ensure the rubric matches outcomes, supports learning, and does not punish students for unclear expectations.

Red flag: AI may create criteria that are too vague, too punitive, or misaligned.

Student-Facing AI Use

AI can help with: brainstorming, practice, revision, and reflection when expectations are clear.

Human review: clarify what students may use AI for, what they must disclose, and what learning still belongs to them.

Red flag: unclear AI policies can create inequity, confusion, or accidental misconduct.

Human Judgment Checklist

  • Is the content accurate?
  • Is it aligned with the course outcomes and assignment purpose?
  • Is it accessible to WCAG 2.1 AA or higher?
  • Does it protect student privacy?
  • Does it avoid stereotypes, bias, or hidden assumptions?
  • Does it make the learning path clearer for students?
  • Would a student know what to do next?

Pause and Apply

Choose one course material you might revise with AI. Before generating anything, write down:

  • What problem am I trying to solve?
  • What student need am I trying to support?
  • What must I review myself before using the output?
